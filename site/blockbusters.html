Feature No. 03 — Podcasting

Blockbusters

The Web3 PR podcast decoding the playbooks behind the industry's biggest stories — and the page you're reading them on.

By Avia · Tel Aviv · 2025 – 2026

Role
Writer & page designer. Production handled by the MarketAcross team.
Scope
Show positioning, episode titles & decks, guest briefs, and the podcast page on marketacross.com.
Live at
marketacross.com/podcast

Blockbusters is a Web3 PR podcast, and both halves of that phrase usually kill a show on arrival. PR is suspect; Web3 is tired; put them together and most people reach for the skip button. I wrote around that problem. The positioning line — "decoding the playbooks behind the industry's biggest stories" — doesn't promise news. It promises method: how the story got made, who called whom, what the press release left out. That's a promise a journalist, a founder, and a marketer can all accept.

Once the positioning held, the rest of the editorial system fell into place. Episode titles are written like magazine decks, not podcast titles: Meat, Potatoes & Press Releases, The Gravity of Growth, The Decentralization Wake-Up Call. Each one names a tension rather than a topic, so the listener knows what argument they've signed up for before the intro ends. The show notes carry the same weight as the cover art — I treat them as the first thirty seconds of the episode, read silently, on a phone, by someone who is deciding whether to press play.

The page you're looking at is part of the same editorial system. The hero is the tagline, not a stock photo of a microphone. The episode index reads like a contents page because that's what a contents page is for. The guest wall is there to let the roster speak without a quote block telling you how to feel about it. Every design decision — the hairline rules, the rust accents, the small-caps platform links — was a copy decision first. I wrote the page the way I'd write a brief, and then set it in type.

A podcast page isn't a list of episodes. It's a contract with the listener: here's what we think is worth your attention, and here's how we'll spend it.

Featured episode

Meat, Potatoes & Press Releases

Behind every Web3 headline is a messy dance between PR and journalists — a sharp, funny look at how stories get made, and how they fall apart.

EP 03 · December 1, 2025 · With Liam J. Kelly, DeFi Correspondent at DLNews

The pitch to Liam Kelly at DLNews was a one-page angle doc, not a booking request. The question underneath the question was: would a working journalist go on a PR firm's podcast and say, on the record, how the press release actually moves through the newsroom? I wrote the angle around a promise — no gotchas, no defensiveness, both chairs kept honest — and built the run-of-show to protect it. The episode title came before the recording. Naming it Meat, Potatoes & Press Releases told Liam what kind of conversation he was agreeing to, and told the eventual listener the same thing. By the time the mics were on, the hard editorial work was already done.

Every episode below was built the same way. A pitch that names the tension a guest is privately wrestling with, a title that promises a specific argument, a question we ask that the guest has never been asked in quite those words, and a closing line we choose before we write the show notes. The Gravity of Growth started from "what actually pulls a crypto project into orbit, and what's just PR." The Decentralization Wake-Up Call started from "when does trustless still need trust." The method is visible in the titles; the work is everything that sits under them.

Feature No. 03 — Podcasting

Building Blockbusters

On the harder thing: making Web3 B2B listenable.

By Avia · Producer & Writer · Tel Aviv · 2024 – 2026

Most Web3 B2B podcasts sound like a quarterly earnings call held inside a server rack. Men in headphones trading acronyms, a logo loop, a link in the bio. I started Blockbusters because I thought the story behind the story — the PR calls, the journalist fights, the product decisions no one tweets about — was the interesting part. The brief I wrote myself was one sentence: make an industry that talks in jargon sound like people talking.

I built the show end-to-end. Format first: conversational, two-voice, thirty to forty minutes, one guest per episode, never a panel. Then the calendar — a rolling two-month window with a warm bench of guests so we could swap when news broke. Then the production flow: intake brief, host prep doc, run-of-show, post-interview trim notes, show notes, three social cuts, a landing page. I ran it from a kitchen in Tel Aviv with a small rotating crew, and the thing I kept tightest was the writing.

Guest wrangling was its own job. The pitch wasn't "come on our podcast" — it was a one-page angle doc that told a CEO what story we thought they were sitting on and why only they could tell it. Charles d'Haussy at dYdX came in to talk about the paradox of trust in a trustless system. Tom Mizzone at Sweet came in to talk about fandom as ownership. Liam Kelly at DLNews came in to talk about the messy dance between PR people and journalists — which is the episode we're all secretly proud of, because a journalist agreed, on the record, to explain how the sausage gets made.

Narrative isn't decoration in B2B. It's the compression algorithm — the only way a stranger remembers what you do.

The script shape mattered more than anything else. I don't write out interviews, but I do write the architecture: a cold open that earns the next twenty minutes, three questions the audience cares about framed as one question the guest wants to answer, a turn where we ask the thing they wouldn't say in a press release, and a close that gives the listener something to do on Monday morning. Hosts got a two-page brief the night before. I sat behind the glass and passed notes.

What this taught me

Producing a podcast is really producing a position. You're deciding, every week, what a company thinks is worth saying and who it sounds like when it says it. That's the same muscle as a brand campaign, a launch narrative, or a founder's keynote — just run at twenty-six episodes a year instead of one.

Seven episodes in, the show has a voice and a shelf — guests pitch us now instead of the other way round. I'm most proud of the line between what I wrote and what ended up in people's ears, because in audio you can hear every seam. The writing is the seam.
