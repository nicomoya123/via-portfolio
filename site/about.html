A writer who ships.

By Avia · Tel Aviv · Spring 2026

I trained as a screenwriter, which is a useful way to say I was taught to think in scenes — who is in the room, what they want, what the shape of the thing is before anyone says a word. The rest has been figuring out what happens when you point that muscle at other surfaces. A brand is a show with no episodes. A product page is a monologue. A podcast is a room you build for a guest to arrive in. I write, mostly, but writing turned out to be a narrow word for the work.

Somewhere along the way I stopped handing briefs off and started producing them. A shop I'd written the positioning for needed a supplier, so I sourced one. A podcast I was writing needed a host brief and a guest pipeline, so I built both. A video series for a research institute needed someone who could sit with a professor at 9am and turn a paper into a cold open by lunchtime, so I did that too. The pattern wasn't planned; it just turned out that narrative tends to die in the handoff, and the person who cares most about a story reaching an audience is usually the one who wrote it down first.

The range on the outside of this issue — a boutique streetwear shop, a Web3 PR podcast, an academic video series, an essay about obituaries — isn't range, really. It's the same muscle pointed at four surfaces. Structure. Voice. A sense of who the audience is and what they have time for. The tools change: a product description is not an interview brief, and neither is a screenplay. The question underneath doesn't: what is the shape of this, and where does the reader actually start.

I'm looking, now, for the next thing that needs both halves — writing and producing, brief and build. Founding narrative or brand roles at companies that are still deciding what they sound like. Editorial lead or writer-producer work on shows, series, and publications that need someone who'll sit with the guest and cut the trailer. Long engagements over sprints. Written work that leaves a product behind.

A writer's note

Three things I keep coming back to.

  • Format is argument. The shape you choose — a 22-minute episode, a product grid of eight, a series of four essays — is already making the case. Pick it on purpose.
  • Write the brief, then build it. A narrative that nobody produces is a pitch deck. The cheapest way to prove a story works is to ship the smallest real version of it.
  • Specific over clever. Proper nouns beat adjectives. "A kitchen in Tel Aviv, 11 at night" is doing more work than any line I could write about craft.
