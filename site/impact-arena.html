Feature No. 01 — Video Series

The Impact Arena

Tel Aviv University’s digital academic platform where research meets reality.

From English-content editor to on-camera presenter · TAU Marketing · 2024–2025

Role
On-camera presenter, co-writer & production assistant.
Also
Pitched topics and researched experts as part of the content & creative team.
Live at
hazira.sites.tau.ac.il

The Impact Arena (הזירה in Hebrew) is Tel Aviv University’s digital platform for making academic research feel current, human, and genuinely engaging. The original format was short-form video: a researcher and a presenter in conversation about current events, politics, culture, and everything in between, bringing voices from every corner of the university to shed light on the moments we’re living through, in the short term and the long. The goal is to build sharper critical thinking, and more open conversation about Israeli society.

I was hired as TAU’s English content editor: website copy, article translation, but I kept pitching creative ideas into the room, and the room kept taking them. I moved from writing social briefs to scripting episodes I wasn’t in, and eventually the team asked if I’d step in front of the camera. I ended up inside the small in-house production unit making the Impact Arena: not because I’d been hired to present, but because I kept showing up with ideas.

The work sat exactly where I wanted to be: academia on one side, current events and pop culture on the other. The craft was mostly in the preparation: building a one-page angle doc; then a short list of questions, in order, that each unlocked the next; shaping those into talking points, and turning the best sixty seconds into the episode. On filming days, my job was to get those talking points out into the room, and to get the researcher comfortable enough to say the thing they’d normally save for the hallway after the panel. Every cut had to leave them sounding like themselves: natural, not lecturing.

Over time the format itself evolved from seated conversations with researchers toward shorter, direct-to-camera pieces built for a younger audience scrolling fast. Developing those new formats became part of the work.

The prep was the part I loved. Reading a researcher until I found the one angle only they could answer.
