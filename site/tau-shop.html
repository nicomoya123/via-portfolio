Feature No. 02 — Brand & Commerce

The Making of  TAU's Merch Store

The Official Tel Aviv University e-commerce website.

Founding operator · TAU Marketing · 2024–2025

Role
Founding operator. Owned the product end to end, from brief to launch.
Scope
Product management, product marketing, UX/UI, copywriting, vendor sourcing, shipping pipeline.
Live at
shop.tau.ac.il

I never thought I’d end up running a shop. I came in as a content person, no e-commerce analytics, no dev agencies, no factory floors, but that’s the point. It’s a reminder of how far content and marketing can take you when the brief is wide enough. Most universities had a proper merch shop. TAU didn’t. The marketing team decided I was the person to close the gap: build the site, develop the products, take it to market. But before any of that, I wanted to answer the question: why should this shop exist at all, and what would it mean for one to actually feel like TAU?

When the TAU President himself noticed and complimented the work, that was the surest sign we’d done it right.

TAU has a distinct voice, and the shop had to hold several audiences at once: students on campus, alumni, prospective candidates, and the wider circle of supporters and partners watching from abroad. After studying every other Israeli university shop and a few Ivy League ones, my brief to myself was a single line: not your regular merch store. TAU is Tel Avivian: diverse, open-minded, forward-leaning, and at the same time a serious interdisciplinary research university. The shop had to feel like both, or it wouldn’t feel like TAU at all.

Not your regular merch store

I planned the shop, project-managed the design team on identity and naming, briefed the vendors, sat across from the Shopify dev agency, went to the printing factory to see the first shirts come off the press, planned the launch on socials, and guided the content team on the reels and posts that introduced it. What mattered to me was keeping TAU’s character intact: Tel Avivian, confident, a little different from the rest, across every surface the shop touched.

The shop opened with two collections, each with its own logic. Essentials was the classic merch lane, but we didn’t want another campus product with a crest and the words Tel Aviv University shouted across the front. So we worked with a fragment of the logo, stripped it back, and let the design stay minimal: not trying to make anyone read the name, trusting that whoever gets it, gets it. TAU X GELADA was the other lane: a capsule with a south Tel Aviv art studio called Gelada, drawn exclusively for us. The collection reads like a storyboard: a single character moves through the city, scene by scene, until they arrive at Sourasky Library on campus, the idea that wherever you go in Tel Aviv, the university is always home.

The official launch landed exactly the way I’d hoped. We had dozens of orders in the first week from students, alumni, faculty, and prospective candidates, and I packed and managed every single one myself. On TAU’s Open Day we ran a pop-up on campus, and the compliments didn’t stop: candidates’ parents compared us to Harvard and NYU, employees wanted one of everything. But when the TAU President himself noticed and complimented the work, that was the surest sign we'd done it right. That was the bar I’d set for myself, and we cleared it. A shop that speaks to one audience well is a merch store. A shop that speaks to three in the same voice is a brand.

what i took with me

TAU Shop taught me everything I didn’t know I’d need about branding, project management, user experience, working with developers and printers, writing briefs that non-writers can read. Above all, it taught me how to take something that begins as an idea and walk it through every stage until it becomes a functioning, successful system. I discovered I enjoy this kind of work far more than I expected to, and it changed the way I think about my own practice. The shop isn’t just a shop for me, it’s a real product I helped build, one that carries an emotional connection between a university and the community around it. And, honestly, it only felt like the beginning.
